Sitter
Experiment in descriptive fiction.

The dog is asleep in the corner. The baby is in my lap. We're watching a movie.

She's four months old. Yellow peach fuzz covers her head. Her skin is so fair it's almost translucent. She's much less of a newborn now than the last time I saw her. Today she made full eye contact for the first time. Her round blue eyes held my gaze.

It's you again, they said. Whadja do with my Moms?

My shoulders are spotted with drool stains. My shirt smells of spit-up formula.

The movie, Baby Bach, is a 30-minute montage of high-end children's toys in action, cut to a MIDI version of Johann Sebastian Bach concertos.

The baby's mother, a Broadway musician, complained about this. We talked about re-dubbing the film to a more respectable classical recording. Still, the DVD is visual and auditory cocaine. Baby and I are mesmerized. Puppies flip, tiny action figures skate, horns blow, trains choo choo, and plastic penguins go down a slide, all in time to music.

The dog lets out a muffled bark. I look over at him. He's still asleep, but his eyes move rapidly under his lids. His breath comes in puffs. His paws jerk. I imagine he's dreaming he's in the park, chasing a ball, or confronting another dog.

Today I am responsible for these two creatures, if only for a few hours.
