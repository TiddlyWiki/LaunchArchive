Tiddlywiki



Hypertext. Rupert the Bear. Three levels: cartoon; short story; longer story. That's hypertext, all on one page. Digital hypertext, which as been around for yonks, is one up by being able to present what you need or hide what you don't want. Reading text without clicking links comes to a dead end unless your go back to the home page.

The weblog had always seemed to have something missing: although there are hyperlinks (web) there is no hypertext. Not true anymore: Tiddlywiki.

An example is worth three pages of explanation :

Elie Springer

In the world of web design, taste is everything. The too-ing and fro-ing of the tiddlywiki might seem too distracting for some. There is an argument for simple linearity. Having used and benefited from hypertext software starting with Hypershell, I recognise the value of this new hyertext. its all about dtata retrieval and fluidity. In the days of hypshell (long gone) it was easy to develop a story without worrying too muchabout the structure AND it was very easty to find keywords or strings of text.


Whether you feel comfortable reading Tiddywiki style webogs it is another yoke, cleidoic.


Tiddlywiki a reusable non-linear personal web notebook

Another example:

Singularity!

One of the confusions with Tiddlywiki formats: hypertext is only distinguished from hyperlinks (web) by the lack of an underline. In practice most surfers will not mind been drawn away from the site to a further webpages. A hypertext text link usually sets the requested hypertext below the one you are reading, and eachhypertext can be closed if you feel there is too much on the page. Though I haven't worked it out yet, it seems you can order the hypertexts rather like one might order the letters in one of those two-dimensional puzzles with one squaure missing which alws the lettrs to be moved around. Once order, it would be possible, for example, to print only the texts one was interested in.

It won't take more than a few minutes to see the advantages of a hypertext weblog over a hyperlink only weblog format. Distinguish between benefits to site creator and user.

Websites, as a genus, have always badly designed because developers cannot underatnd the users needs. A profusion of cramped links; too many colours; unnecessary use of several fonts: these are still commonplace, despite lorry-loads of books on how to make an effective website.
