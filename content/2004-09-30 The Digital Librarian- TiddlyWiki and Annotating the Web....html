TiddlyWiki and Annotating the Web...

There's a fellow in London who has done an ingenius thing - he has created a totally client-side wiki tool. TiddlyWiki is an amazing display of creativity - go now and play with it, and then return to read the rest of this post.

So, now that you've TiddlyWikied (I'm hoping that's a correct usage of the term), think about how such a tool might be used in a research setting. The W3C has a project looking at enhancing collaboration on the web through the sharing of "metadata based web annotations, bookmarks, and their combinations." Their approach to web-based annotation collaboration is interesting, but in my mind, a bit too involved at this point to fall into the 'easily adoptable' bucket. However, think about a service like Unalog. One of Unalog's strength's is its low barrier to adoptability. It's extremely easy to click on a Unalog bookmarklet, add (or not) a description to the link, and create a linkstack. Now, let's take this a step further: Imagine that, once you've stacked a link in Unalog, you then had not only the ability to return to the page you just stacked, but you also had the option to return to the page you just stacked wrapped in TiddlyWiki functionality. In other words, you could immediately start annotating *any* html-driven web page. Better yet, you could then perhaps save the annotated version of that page to the Unalog server, linking it to the original linkstack. You could then not only share your linkstacks, but your annotations of the linkstacked resource, in the context of the resource. Unalog and TiddlyWiki seem to be a perfect match for each other, if the wrapping ability I mention above is feasible. My gut tells me it is, but I guess we'll have to wait until someone tries it out (hackfest idea, anyone?)
